Responsible AI for Leaders

Preread 3 · wrapper  •  ~1 hr  •  Google — Introduction to Responsible AI
Why this matters to you

In this program you'll build with real problems and real data from your function. That makes responsible use non-optional — and as a sponsor, you are the one accountable for how AI behaves on your team, not the vendor.

By the end you'll be able to spot the three risks that actually bite in practice — wrong data going in, biased or false output coming out, no human checking — and name a guardrail for each.

The frame

"Responsible AI" sounds like a compliance topic. For a leader it's simpler and more practical: it's the small set of habits that keep an AI tool from embarrassing you, misleading your team, or leaking something it shouldn't. Three risks carry most of the weight.

The three risks that actually bite

RiskWhat it looks likeYour guardrail
Data leakageConfidential info pasted into a tool that may store or train on itA clear rule on what data can / can't go in (we set one in Week 0)
BiasOutput that quietly disadvantages a group — in hiring, scoring, wordingTest on varied cases; never auto-decide on people without review
Unchecked outputA confident, wrong answer acted on as if verifiedHuman-in-the-loop on anything with real consequences

Carry these three away

  • Accountability is human. "The AI did it" is never a defence — the sponsor owns the outcome.
  • Transparency & explainability — be able to say why a tool produced what it did, especially for decisions about people.
  • Guardrails beat good intentions. A written data rule and a review step do more than telling people to "be careful."
