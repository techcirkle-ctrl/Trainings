Section 1 of 6~20 min

How generative AI predicts

Why this matters to you

Before you sponsor a tool built on this, you need a gut feel for what it's actually doing — because that explains both its magic and its one dangerous failure mode.

Traditional software follows rules a person wrote. Generative AI doesn't follow rules — it predicts. It learned the patterns in a vast amount of text, and it writes by repeatedly guessing the most plausible next chunk of words. That's the whole trick. Try it:

Build a sentence, one prediction at a time

Each word it adds becomes part of the input for guessing the next one. Click a bar to pick that word yourself, or let it predict.

The two things to take from that

What you sawWhat it means for you
It chose from probabilities, and "Sample" can give a different sentence each runIt's probabilistic — great for drafting and ideas, awkward when you need one exact, repeatable answer
It never looked anything up — it generated plausible wordsFluency ≠ accuracy. A confident, well-written answer can still be invented. This is the failure mode to manage

Inside an LLM

Why this matters to you

Knowing the engine's two hard limits is what lets you tell a realistic AI proposal from a fantasy one when your team brings you ideas. Everything else this week exists to work around these two limits.

An LLM learns by reading enormous amounts of text and predicting the next token (a word-chunk). Two ideas explain why it's so capable — and exactly where it falls short.

Section 3 of 6~25 min

The AI Evolution Stack

Why this matters to you

Every AI initiative you'll ever sponsor sits on one of four layers. Naming the lowest layer that solves a problem is the single most useful judgement an AI-capable leader has — it decides cost, risk, and whether the thing works. This is the backbone of the whole program.

Each layer adds one capability the one below it lacks. You only climb when the layer below genuinely can't do the job. Click each rung to open it.

Layer 1 · LLM

A reasoning engine ▾

Reads and writes language, reasons over what it's given. On its own it's brilliant but blind to your business and forgets every conversation.

In your function: draft a customer email, summarise a report you paste in, brainstorm options.
⤒ can't see your live data, and can't do anything beyond producing text.
Layer 2 · RAG

The engine, grounded in your documents ▾

Fetches the relevant pieces of your own content at the moment of the question, so answers are grounded and can cite their source.

In your function: “Answer only from our latest pricing & policy docs,” with the source shown.
⤒ still only answers — it doesn't take actions or complete multi-step work.
Layer 3 · Agent

The engine, given tools and a goal ▾

An LLM that can use tools, take steps in a loop, and keep state — look something up, act, check the result, continue — until a goal is met. Where AI stops answering and starts doing work.

In your function: “Take an inbound RFP, pull the right specs, draft a quote, flag anything needing my sign-off.”
⤒ one agent juggling many specialised jobs gets unreliable and hard to govern.
Layer 4 · Multi-Agent

A team of specialised agents, coordinated ▾

Several focused agents coordinated by an orchestrator. Powerful for genuinely complex workflows — and the layer with the highest cost, latency, and governance burden. Most problems never need it.

In your function: a full deal-desk — one agent researches, one prices, one checks compliance, one drafts.
⤒ complexity and cost — reach here only when a single agent provably can't cope.

The leader's decision lens

If the problem is…You need at most…
Generate / rewrite / summarise text I give itLLM
Answer questions from our own documents, with sourcesRAG
Complete a multi-step job that takes actions in toolsAgent
Coordinate several distinct specialised jobs at onceMulti-Agent

Rule of thumb: the cheapest layer that works, wins. Reaching too high is the most common and most expensive mistake.

    Section 5 of 6~25 min
    
RAG — grounding in your documents

    
Why this matters to you

      
"It doesn't know our business" was Limit 1 from Section 2. RAG is the fix — and the most common, lowest-risk way to get real value from AI on your own content. You'll see one built live in the session.


    
RAG = Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Instead of trusting the model's frozen memory, you retrieve the relevant pieces of your documents at question time and hand them over — so the answer is grounded and cites its source. Step through it:

The contrast is the whole point: without RAG, the model fills the gap with a plausible-sounding guess. With RAG, it answers only from what's retrieved — and if the answer isn't in your documents, a well-built RAG system says so instead of inventing.
