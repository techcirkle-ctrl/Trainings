Section 1 of 6~20 min

How generative AI predicts

Why this matters to you

Before you sponsor a tool built on this, you need a gut feel for what it's actually doing — because that explains both its magic and its one dangerous failure mode.

Traditional software follows rules a person wrote. Generative AI doesn't follow rules — it predicts. It learned the patterns in a vast amount of text, and it writes by repeatedly guessing the most plausible next chunk of words. That's the whole trick. Try it:

Build a sentence, one prediction at a time
Our customer was very
Each word it adds becomes part of the input for guessing the next one. Click a bar to pick that word yourself, or let it predict.

The dial you just felt: temperature

"Take the most likely word" vs "Sample" wasn't a toy — it's a real control called temperature, the creativity dial. Low temperature → it almost always takes the top word: consistent, repeatable. High temperature → it samples more widely: more varied and creative, less predictable. Drag it, then generate a few times and watch the spread:

The creativity dial — drag, then generate
Low High
Our customer was very …
Low temperature picks the top word almost every time — consistent. Crank it up and it samples wider — more variety, more risk.

Leader's read: turn temperature down for anything that must be consistent and factual; up for brainstorming and first drafts. It's a knob your teams will set on the tools they build.

So where do those percentages come from?

Nobody programmed "after very, the word happy is 34% likely." The model learned it — by reading an enormous amount of text (books, articles, code, the public web) and noticing, billions of times over, which words tend to follow which. That body of text is its training data, and those learned tendencies are the percentages you just clicked. Three things fall straight out of that — each one changes how you should use it:

  • It's only as current as its training. Anything that happened — or changed in your business — after training simply isn't in there. (That gap is Section 2's first limit.)
  • It inherits its sources. Whatever patterns, blind spots and biases live in the text it learned from, it absorbs. Good reason to check, not assume.
  • It generates, it never retrieves. It isn't looking anything up in a database — it's predicting plausible text. That single fact explains the failure mode below.

It predicts in chunks called tokens

One more piece of plumbing worth knowing, because it's the unit everything is priced and measured in. The model doesn't read whole words — it reads tokens: common chunks of text. Short words are one token; longer ones split into several. It's literally guessing the next chunk, over and over:

One word ≠ one token
Our customer was very frustrated
Roughly ¾ of a word per token. When you hear "context window" or "cost per million tokens" later, this is the unit they mean.

The one failure mode that actually bites

Because it generates plausible text rather than checking facts, it can be completely confident and completely wrong — a hallucination. The trap: it writes just as fluently when it's wrong as when it's right. Test your own instinct — which of these would you trust as-is?

Which would you trust?
Make a call on each — then see the catch.

The three things to take from that

What you sawWhat it means for you
It chose from probabilities, and higher temperature gives a different sentence each runIt's probabilistic — great for drafting and ideas, something to control (turn temperature down) when you need one exact, repeatable answer
The percentages were learned from training data, frozen at training timeIt's blind to anything recent or internal to your business — the gap Section 2 names and RAG later closes

Section 2 of 6~25 min

Inside an LLM

Why this matters to you

Knowing the engine's two hard limits is what lets you tell a realistic AI proposal from a fantasy one when your team brings you ideas. Everything else this week exists to work around these two limits.

An LLM learns by reading enormous amounts of text and predicting the next token (a word-chunk). Underneath the chat box it's a single machine — a vast network of numbers. We'll open it up, then meet the two ideas that explain why it's so capable, and exactly where it falls short.

First — what the engine actually is

Strip away the chat box and an LLM is one machine: a huge network of simple units (loosely, "neurons") wired together in layers. Your words enter on the left; each layer passes numbers to the next; a prediction comes out on the right. The wires carry weights — numbers saying how much one unit's signal counts toward the next. A real model has billions of them. Press Run to send a prompt through — and click any neuron to see what it actually does:

A tiny network, predicting the next word
Press Run to watch the signal flow, or click a neuron to inspect its connections.

Leader's read: nothing is stored in here like a database — there are only these weights. "Training" means setting them, and the model's entire knowledge is that pattern of numbers. Hold that picture: it explains both why it's so fluent and why it can be confidently wrong.

1. Attention — how it "knows what you mean"

When the model reads a word, it weighs which earlier words matter most for understanding it. That's attention. Click any word below to see what it leans on:

Click a word — especially “it” — to see which earlier words the model attends to.

Leader's read: attention is why the model handles pronouns, long sentences and cross-references so fluently — and why a clearer prompt genuinely helps. The less ambiguous the words you give it, the less it has to guess what refers to what. Vague input doesn't break it; it just makes it guess.

2. The context window — its working memory

The model can only "hold in mind" what's in the current conversation — the context window. Fill it past its limit and the earliest content drops off. Watch it happen:

Window holds 8 chunks. Keep adding — see what falls out.

This toy window holds 8 chunks. A real one holds far more — today's larger models hold the equivalent of several hundred pages at once. But three consequences follow, and each one costs you money or accuracy if ignored:

  • Everything shares the one window. Your instructions, every document you paste, the whole back-and-forth, and the answer all compete for the same space.
  • Long chats quietly forget. In a marathon conversation the earliest turns fall out of the window — which is exactly why an assistant can "lose the thread" halfway through.
  • You pay per token, every turn. The context is re-sent with each message, so a giant window is powerful but never free.

Leader's read: for big or numerous documents, retrieving the relevant pieces (Section 5's RAG) usually beats stuffing everything into the window — cheaper, and the model stays focused on what actually matters.

3. How it learned — and how it gets tuned

Two stages, worth separating because teams constantly confuse them — and the confusion drives bad proposals:

  • Pre-training — the model reads a vast, general body of text and learns language, facts and reasoning patterns. Expensive, done once, by the model maker. This is the knowledge that's "frozen at training time."
  • Fine-tuning — a much smaller, targeted pass that shapes behaviour (tone, format, instruction-following, staying safe) — not fresh facts. When someone says "let's fine-tune it on our data," the honest question is: do you need new facts (that's RAG) or new behaviour (that's fine-tuning)? They get mixed up constantly, and picking wrong wastes months.

So what is "learning"? Just nudging those billions of weights to make better predictions. Feed the network examples and watch the error fall:

Watch it "learn" — every example nudges the numbers

Examples read: 0

One more lever: which model

Not every job needs the biggest model. You're trading three things against each other, and defaulting to "the largest, newest one" for everything is one of the most common and most expensive habits:

Bigger / newer modelSmaller / faster model
Hard reasoningStronger on ambiguous, multi-step problemsCan stumble on the genuinely tricky ones
Speed & costSlower, more expensive per tokenFaster and far cheaper at volume
Reach for it when…Complex analysis, nuanced drafting, ambiguous tasksHigh-volume, simple, well-defined tasks

Leader's read: match the model to the job, not to its reputation. A cheap model that's clearly good enough, run thousands of times, beats an expensive one used out of habit. You'll meet the Claude family (Haiku / Sonnet / Opus tiers) and when to reach for each in the session.

Section 3 of 6~25 min

The AI Evolution Stack

Why this matters to you

Almost every AI initiative you'll sponsor is some combination of a few capabilities bolted onto a language engine. Knowing which ones a problem actually needs — and which it doesn't — is the single most useful judgement an AI-capable leader has. It decides cost, risk, and whether the thing works.

An LLM is the engine. Everything people call "the stack" is really a set of capabilities you bolt on — by need, not by rank. Two independent questions decide what you add:

  • Does it need to know your world? Your documents, your data. If yes, give it retrieval — that's RAG.
  • Does it need to act? Use tools, take steps, not just talk. If yes, wrap it in a tool-using loop — that's an agent.

These are independent: add neither, either, or both. An agent might use retrieval — or act purely on live systems with no documents at all. A RAG assistant needn't take a single action. And when one agent juggling everything gets unreliable, you split the work across several coordinated agents (multi-agent). Assemble a system and see what you get:

Two things to take from that. One: the capabilities compose — there's no fixed order, and "agent" does not imply "RAG." Two: the cost bar climbs with every capability you switch on, so the leader's discipline is simple — add the fewest capabilities that actually solve the problem. Over-building is the most common and most expensive mistake.

RAG — grounding in your documents

    
Why this matters to you

      
"It doesn't know our business" was Limit 1 from Section 2. RAG is the fix — and the most common, lowest-risk way to get real value from AI on your own content. You'll see one built live in the session.


    
RAG = Retrieval-Augmented Generation — the fix for "it doesn't know our business." Instead of trusting the model's frozen memory, you retrieve the relevant pieces of your own documents at question time and hand them over. Grounded answers that cite their source — and admit when they don't know. Don't take that on faith. Drive it yourself:

What you just watched — the three steps, every time

    • 
    

    
Leader's read: a RAG system lives or dies on the documents behind it. Out-of-date or contradictory sources produce confidently grounded — but wrong — answers. "Set up RAG" is maybe 20% model and 80% getting your content clean, current and well-organised.

Agents vs. chatbots — and using them responsibly

    
Why this matters to you

      
An agent is the thing you'll build and deploy in this program. And because you'll build it on real data, responsible use isn't a compliance box — it's how you avoid leaking, misleading, or embarrassing your team. Both live here.


    
A chatbot answers. An agent does the work.

    
Give an agent a goal (not a question); it uses tools, takes steps in a loop, and tracks where it is until the goal is met. Step the agent and watch the loop:

What makes the loop work

    
Four parts are what turn a chatbot into an agent — the same four you just stepped through:

    
    
      
  • Goal — an outcome to reach, not a single question to answer.
    • 
      
  • Tools — ways to act and look things up: read a system, query a database, send a draft.
    • 
      
  • Loop — act → observe the result → decide the next step, repeated.
    • 
      
  • Stopping condition — how it knows it's done, and when to hand back to a human.
    • 
    


    
Is it actually worth an agent?

    
An agent earns its extra cost and risk only when the task is all four of these:

    
    
      
  • Multi-step — not a single answer a chatbot already gives you.
    • 
      
  • Acts in tools — it has to do things, not just write.
    • 
      
  • Repeats often — frequent enough to be worth building and maintaining.
    • 
      
  • Has clear rules for what "done" and "escalate" mean.
    • 
    

    
Leader's read: more autonomy isn't automatically better. The real design question is where the human sits — reviewing every action, or only the consequential ones? That single choice is most of your risk management. Miss the clear rules and you get an unreliable agent; build one for a rare task and you've over-engineered.
