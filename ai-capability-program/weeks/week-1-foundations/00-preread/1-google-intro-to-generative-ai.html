What Generative AI Actually Is

Preread 1 · wrapper  •  ~1 hr  •  Google Cloud — Introduction to Generative AI
Why this matters to you

You are about to sponsor tools built on generative AI and put them in front of your team. Before you can judge where it's safe to use, you need to know what it is — and, just as important, what it is not reliable at.

By the end you'll be able to tell, for a task in your own function, whether it's the kind of thing generative AI does well — or the kind where it will confidently get things wrong.

The frame

Traditional software follows rules a person wrote. Generative AI doesn't follow rules — it predicts. Trained on vast amounts of text and images, it learns the patterns in that data and then produces new content — a paragraph, a summary, an image — by predicting what most plausibly comes next.

That one fact explains both its magic and its failure mode. It is astonishing at producing fluent, plausible drafts. It has no built-in sense of true vs. false — only likely. The course below gives you the full picture; these are the three things to hold onto.

Generative vs. traditional, in one table

Traditional softwareGenerative AI
Follows explicit rules a human codedPredicts patterns it learned from data
Same input → same output, every timeSame input → can vary; it's probabilistic
Breaks loudly when it can't do somethingOften "succeeds" plausibly even when wrong

That last row is the leader's whole risk picture: the danger isn't that it fails obviously — it's that it can be confidently wrong.

Carry these three away

  • It generates, it doesn't retrieve. Output is newly predicted text, not a looked-up fact — so it can invent.
  • It's probabilistic. Ask twice, get two phrasings. Useful for drafting, awkward where you need one exact answer.
  • Fluency ≠ accuracy. A confident, well-written answer can still be wrong. Always true; matters most with real stakes.
