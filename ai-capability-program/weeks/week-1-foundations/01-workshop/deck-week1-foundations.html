“The trick is simpler than you'd think.”

  


  

    
The whole trick — two minutes
It predicts the next word. Then it does it again.

      

        
What it does
Very good autocomplete
Trained on vast text, it learned which words follow which in context. Give it the start of a sentence → it predicts the next word → feeds that back → predicts again — until done. No database. No lookup. Pure sophisticated prediction.

        
The dial
Temperature — how surprising
Low (precise): takes the safest word almost every time — same answer 5 times in a row. High (creative): surprising words get a real shot — 5 different results. Your team can tune this per task.

      

      The implication: it generates — it doesn't retrieve. Nothing "stays" between responses. That's exactly why constraining what it says (Act 1 prompting) and grounding it in your documents (Act 3 RAG) are non-optional.

    
Takeaway
An LLM predicts the next word, feeds it back, and repeats — sophisticated autocomplete. It generates, it doesn't look things up. Temperature is the one dial: low = repeatable, high = creative.

    
Move

Say
“It predicts the next word. That's it. Trained on a vast amount of text, it learned which words tend to follow which, in context. You give it a start, it predicts the next word, feeds that back, predicts again — and loops until done. A poem, code, a strategy memo — all the same loop. No database, no lookup; it's generating, not retrieving.”

      
“The one dial is temperature. Low, and it takes the safe word every time — the same answer five times over. High, and surprising words get a shot — five different results. And the implication matters: because it generates rather than looks things up, nothing ‘stays’ between answers — which is exactly why constraining it in Act 1, and grounding it in your documents in Act 3, are not optional.”
