Section 2 of 6~12 min

System prompts that hold under pressure

Why this matters to you

Your agent's system prompt is its constitution — the rules it operates under every single run. A vague prompt produces a compliant agent in good conditions and an unreliable one under pressure: ambiguous inputs, missing data, edge cases. This section shows you exactly what each layer of a system prompt does — so when yours breaks, you know which layer to fix.

Section 3 of 6~12 min

Workflows, chains, and conditional logic

Why this matters to you

Most of what looks like "one smart agent" is actually several smaller pieces working in sequence — and that's on purpose. Splitting a task into a chain is often cheaper, faster, and easier to govern than building one monolithic agent. But splitting too aggressively adds cost and complexity with no gain. This section gives you the intuition for when to split — and when not to.

↩ From Week 2: you learned the spectrum from workflow (you define the path) to agent (the model defines the path). This week goes one level deeper — inside a workflow or agent, you can chain sub-steps, add conditional branches, and link specialists in sequence. The procurement-compliance agent below is this week's worked example — now watch it split.

The same task can be handled three ways: by one agent doing everything, by a linear chain of specialist steps, or with conditional logic that branches on what it finds. Pick a mode and step through what happens — then check the cost and tradeoffs:

Leader's read

The session's Hour 3 shows you how to wire these patterns in Claude Projects — no code, using sequential prompts and knowledge documents. Your decision: how many steps does my task genuinely need, and does the path ever branch? That determines which pattern earns its complexity.

💬 You raised this on Friday — "multi-agent," and one portal where all our agents talk to each other. Worth an honest answer. A chain of specialists — one agent's output handed to the next — is what "multi-agent" usually means at this stage, and you just stepped through it above. True orchestration (agents calling other agents as tools) multiplies token cost fast: every handoff is more tool calls, more tokens, more to govern — which is why most enterprises haven't put it into production. It's a Week 4 / production topic, not this week's build. One leader put it exactly right in the room: "what we need is a workflow, not an agent." Start there — reach for orchestration only when a single agent or a simple chain genuinely can't do the job.

Section 4 of 6~12 min

Connecting agents to knowledge

Why this matters to you

An agent that can't look things up makes things up. Grounding — connecting your agent to documents it can actually read — is what separates a useful, trustworthy agent from a confident-sounding one that fabricates policy, figures, or facts. As the leader, you decide what knowledge your agent has access to — and you own what happens when it gets something wrong.

Two paths — and you're only on one of them. Your path is simpler and just as real: load your supplier policy, HR guidelines, or approval framework into Claude Projects → Knowledge, no code. ⚠ A trap you'll likely hit in the sprint — big structured files. A leader loaded a ~1,000-row SharePoint tracker into Project knowledge and got back search snippets, not rows — the agent couldn't see the whole table. Large spreadsheets and trackers aren't a good fit for Project knowledge: it retrieves passages, not full datasets. If your use case needs one, split it, summarise it down to what matters, or use the tool / connector path the trainer shows — don't just paste the whole file in and trust it.

Every document you don't load is a gap the agent may fill with a confident guess. Your job is to decide: what must it always know? Load those. Treat anything else as a lookup risk you're accepting.

Section 6 of 6~12 min

How RAG works — the open-book exam

Why this matters to you

In Section 4 you loaded a document and the agent gave a grounded answer. RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is the machine behind that — how an agent finds the right passage in a big pile of documents instead of reading everything or guessing. You won't wire this yourself, but you sponsor it, you fund it, and you'll watch the trainer run it live on Friday. This primer means that won't be your first look.

Leader's read

You don't build the pipeline — but every stage is a decision you sponsor: what goes in the library, how fresh it's kept, whether the agent is told to say "I don't know," and how you'll know the answers are trustworthy. RAG turns "the agent sounds confident" into "the agent can show you the source." That's the difference you're paying for.
