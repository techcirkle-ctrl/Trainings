Section 2 of 5~12 min

System prompts that hold under pressure

Why this matters to you

Your agent's system prompt is its constitution — the rules it operates under every single run. A vague prompt produces a compliant agent in good conditions and an unreliable one under pressure: ambiguous inputs, missing data, edge cases. This section shows you exactly what each layer of a system prompt does — so when yours breaks, you know which layer to fix.

Section 3 of 5~12 min

Workflows, chains, and conditional logic

Why this matters to you

Most of what looks like "one smart agent" is actually several smaller pieces working in sequence — and that's on purpose. Splitting a task into a chain is often cheaper, faster, and easier to govern than building one monolithic agent. But splitting too aggressively adds cost and complexity with no gain. This section gives you the intuition for when to split — and when not to.

↩ From Week 2: you learned the spectrum from workflow (you define the path) to agent (the model defines the path). This week goes one level deeper — inside a workflow or agent, you can chain sub-steps, add conditional branches, and link specialists in sequence. The procurement-compliance agent below is the same one you know — now watch it split.

The same task can be handled three ways: by one agent doing everything, by a linear chain of specialist steps, or with conditional logic that branches on what it finds. Pick a mode and step through what happens — then check the cost and tradeoffs:

Watch the number of steps and the path change across modes. In conditional logic, steps the agent doesn't need are simply skipped — fewer tokens, lower cost, same result.

Section 4 of 5~12 min

Connecting agents to knowledge

Why this matters to you

An agent that can't look things up makes things up. Grounding — connecting your agent to documents it can actually read — is what separates a useful, trustworthy agent from a confident-sounding one that fabricates policy, figures, or facts. As the leader, you decide what knowledge your agent has access to — and you own what happens when it gets something wrong.

Walk into the sprint with your v2 plan written

The two build hours go fast. You don't want to spend the first 20 minutes deciding what to fix. Here's the one thing to do before you walk in:

Open your 1-page design doc and write three lines at the top:

  • What broke — the exact failure mode from the awkward-input test.
  • Which layer to fix — the system-prompt layer, the knowledge gap, or the stopping rule.
  • The one concrete change — not "improve it" but "add this exact guardrail line" or "load this document."

That's your sprint brief. Hand it to the trainer in Hour 4 and they'll build alongside you. In Hour 5, you apply what you learned in Sprint 1 to a second agent — your strongest use case — and demo it to the group.

After the session: your homework is to pick the use case with the most organisational impact, write a v0 specification (users, problem, success criteria, data, guardrails), build the v1 prototype, and get feedback from one colleague. That prototype is your Week 4 capstone input — so the stronger your v2 coming out of today, the less you're building from scratch at the end.

Leader's read: the hardening sprint isn't about polish — it's about trust. A v2 you'd actually put in front of a colleague is the bar. If you wouldn't share it yet, that tells you exactly where to spend the sprint.
