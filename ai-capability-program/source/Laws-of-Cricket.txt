THE LAWS OF CRICKET — A CONCISE REFERENCE

An original, plain-language summary of how cricket is played and governed. Each
section states discrete, self-contained facts, so a question about one topic can
be answered from one passage.


1. THE GAME IN BRIEF

Cricket is a bat-and-ball game played between two teams of eleven players each.
It is played on a grass field with a rectangular strip in the middle called the
pitch. The aim is simple: score more runs than the opposing team.

One team bats while the other bowls and fields. The batting side tries to score
runs; the fielding side tries to limit runs and dismiss the batters. A team's
turn to bat is called an innings. Depending on the format, a match has one or two
innings per side.


2. THE PITCH, THE WICKETS AND THE CREASES

The pitch is 22 yards (20.12 metres) long and 10 feet (3.05 metres) wide.

At each end of the pitch stands a wicket. A wicket is made of three wooden stumps,
each 28 inches (71.1 centimetres) high, with two small wooden crosspieces called
bails resting in grooves on top. The three stumps together are 9 inches (22.86
centimetres) wide.

The popping crease (the batting crease) is marked 4 feet (1.22 metres) in front of
each wicket. A batter is safely "in their ground" when the bat or a part of the
body is grounded behind this line. If the wicket is broken while the batter is out
of their ground, the batter can be dismissed.


3. THE PLAYERS AND THE OFFICIALS

Each team has eleven players. One is named captain and is responsible for the
conduct of the team.

The batting side has two batters at the crease at any time: the striker, who faces
the current delivery, and the non-striker at the bowler's end. The fielding side
has one bowler, one wicket-keeper who stands behind the striker's wicket, and nine
other fielders positioned around the ground.

Two on-field umpires officiate the match. One stands behind the bowler's wicket;
the other stands square of the pitch at square leg. The umpires judge dismissals,
signal runs and extras, and control the game. In many matches a third umpire
reviews close decisions using television replays, and the Decision Review System
(DRS) lets teams challenge a limited number of on-field decisions per innings.


4. THE BALL, THE BAT AND THE EQUIPMENT

A cricket ball has a cork core wound with string and covered in leather with a
raised seam. It weighs between 155.9 and 163 grams and measures about 22.4 to 22.9
centimetres around. A red ball is used in the longest format; a white ball is used
in limited-overs matches.

A cricket bat is made of willow. It may be no more than 38 inches (96.5
centimetres) long and no more than 4.25 inches (10.8 centimetres) wide.

Batters wear protective equipment including pads, gloves and a helmet. The
wicket-keeper wears large gloves and pads.


5. OVERS AND INNINGS

An over consists of six fair (legal) deliveries bowled from one end of the pitch.

After each over, play switches to the other end and a different bowler delivers the
next over. A bowler is not permitted to bowl two consecutive overs in the same
innings. No-balls and wides do not count as one of the six deliveries; they are
bowled again.


6. SCORING RUNS

A run is scored when the two batters run the length of the pitch and exchange ends;
each completed exchange counts as one run. The batters may run more than once from
a single delivery.

A ball that reaches the boundary along the ground scores four runs. A ball that
clears the boundary on the full, without bouncing, scores six runs.

Extras are runs added to the team total without the bat striking the ball. The four
kinds of extra are wides, no-balls, byes and leg byes. A bye is scored when the ball
passes the striker without touching the bat and the batters run. A leg bye is scored
when the ball hits the striker's body (not the bat) and the batters run.


7. THE NO-BALL

A no-ball is an illegal delivery. The most common cause is the bowler overstepping:
some part of the front foot must land behind the popping crease. A delivery is also
a no-ball if the bowler throws (straightens the arm) rather than bowls it, if it is
dangerously high or fast, or if the fielding side is set illegally.

A no-ball costs the fielding side one run and must be bowled again, so it does not
count as one of the six balls in the over. In limited-overs cricket the delivery
after a no-ball is often a free hit, on which the striker cannot be bowled or caught
out.


8. THE WIDE BALL

A wide is a delivery bowled so far from the striker that it cannot be played with a
normal cricket stroke. It costs the fielding side one run and must be bowled again,
so it too does not count as one of the six balls in the over.


9. THE WAYS A BATTER CAN BE DISMISSED (GETTING OUT)

There are ten recognised ways in which a batter can be dismissed, that is, given
out. In order of how often they occur, they are: bowled, caught, leg before wicket,
run out, stumped, hit wicket, hit the ball twice, obstructing the field, handled the
ball, and timed out. In the modern Laws, "handled the ball" has been absorbed into
"obstructing the field," so nine distinct methods now remain. The fielding side must
appeal ("How's that?") before an umpire will give a batter out.

Bowled: the batter is out bowled if the delivery hits the wicket and dislodges at
least one bail, whether it touched the bat first or not. Bowled takes precedence
over every other form of dismissal.

Caught: the batter is out caught if the ball touches the bat and is then caught by a
fielder on the full, before it hits the ground.

Leg before wicket (LBW): the batter is out LBW if the ball would have gone on to hit
the stumps but was stopped by the batter's body, usually the pad, subject to
conditions about where the ball pitched and struck.

Run out: a batter is out run out if the wicket is broken by the fielding side while
that batter is out of their ground attempting a run.

Stumped: the batter is out stumped if the wicket-keeper breaks the wicket while the
batter is out of their ground and not attempting a run.

Hit wicket: the batter is out hit wicket if they dislodge their own bails with the
bat or body while playing the ball or setting off for a run.

Hit the ball twice: the batter is out if they deliberately strike the ball a second
time, other than to guard the wicket.

Obstructing the field: the batter is out if they deliberately obstruct or distract
the fielding side by word or action, including handling the ball without consent.

Timed out: an incoming batter is out if they are not ready to face the next delivery
within the allowed time after the previous wicket fell.


10. THE DEAD BALL

The ball becomes dead when it is finally settled in the hands of the bowler or
wicket-keeper, when a boundary is scored, when a batter is dismissed, or when the
umpire calls "over" or "time." No runs can be scored and no batter can be dismissed
while the ball is dead.


11. THE RESULT OF THE MATCH

The team that scores more runs wins the match. The umpires are responsible for the
correctness of the score.

In the longest format, a side must dismiss the opposition twice, or force a result
within the allotted time, to win; otherwise the match is drawn. In limited-overs
cricket the side batting second wins by passing the first side's total; if the
scores finish level, the match is tied, and a tie-breaker such as a Super Over may
decide it.


12. THE MAIN FORMATS

Test cricket is the longest format, played over up to five days, with two innings
per side and a red ball.

A One Day International (ODI) gives each side one innings of 50 overs, played with a
white ball.

Twenty20 (T20) gives each side one innings of 20 overs, and is the shortest and
fastest format.


13. THE SPIRIT OF CRICKET

Cricket owes much of its appeal to the expectation that it is played not only within
its Laws but also within the Spirit of the Game. Captains are responsible for their
players' conduct. Players are expected to respect their opponents, the umpires and
the traditional values of the game. There should be no unfair play, no dissent at an
umpire's decision, and no violence. The umpire's decision is final and is accepted
without argument.
